Look alike script

The Ground Truth: The A/B Test (96.8K Users)
Definition: A "Static Snapshot" of exactly who we sent the voucher to on Launch Day.
The Baseline: Uses a local Hold-out Group (10.7K users) in the same cities.


Methodology 1: Customer Lookalike (1:1 Matching)
The Audience: 39.9K Users (Precision Focus)
Why the difference? We applied a "Rigor Filter" to exclude low-risk users who would have ordered anyway. By focusing only on the High-Risk (35%+) customers, we remove "organic noise."
The Logic: Uses KNN Matching to find a "Twin" for our customers in a different city (e.g., matching a Dortmund user to a Hannover user).
The Math: It calculates the "distance" between two people’s behaviors. It doesn't care about "trends" or "time series"; it only cares about individual similarity.

 Methodology 2: City Lookalike (Audience-Locked)
The Audience: 98.0K Users (Market Focus)
Why the difference? It is a "Dynamic Query." It captures the 96.8K treated users PLUS ~1.2K users who organically entered the "High-Risk" segment or moved into the city during the campaign.
The Logic: Uses Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS) to compare the entire city's daily sales trend against 22 other German cities to predict a "Counterfactual" baseline.
The Math: It calculates correlations over time. It doesn't care about individual "twins"; it only cares about the relationship between city-wide trends.

Campaign Analysis

Reconciling Our Measurement Models
Why does Customer Look Alike find 96% of the uplift in 40% of the audience?
1. The "Voucher Waste" Discovery (The 57k "Organic" Users)
  
The A/B test (Ground Truth) includes everyone, including 57k users who were "Low-Risk."
The Reality: These 57k users are loyalists. They were going to order anyway. When we send them a voucher, they use it, but they do not drive incremental growth.
The Math: By excluding them with our Rigor Filter (>35% Prob), we are proving that 96% of the campaign's true success came from the "High-Risk" 40k.

2. The "Multiplier" Factor (Eli’s 1.5x Correction)
The Customer Lookalike uses Contamination Multiplier (1.5x)
Why? In the Maverick market (Dortmund/Essen), even our "Lookalikes" are influenced by the campaign's presence (advertising, local hype).
The Correction: The 7.8k total includes a +50% "correction" to account for this contamination.
The Comparison: Without the 1.5x multiplier, the Customer Look alike would only report ~5.2k orders. The multiplier is what brings us into alignment with the "Raw Truth" of the A/B test.

3. Signal-to-Noise Ratio (The City Lookalike Failure)
  Use the City Lookalike result as your counter-example:
The City Lookalike V2 used 98k people (the full city volume).
The Result: It only found 447 incremental orders (a 6% accuracy).
The Lesson: This proves that when you include the "Noise" (the other 58k people), the mathematical model breaks. It can't "see" the 8,100 incremental orders because they are buried under the organic behavior of the 58,000 active users.

Campaign Analysis

Reconciling Our Measurement Models
Why did City Lookalike V1 "over-report" by 750,000 customers?
1. The "Open Geography" Trap (Total City vs. Targeted List)
The Ground Truth (96.8K): This is a "Closed List." We only track the specific people who were sent the voucher.
City Lookalike - Monte Carlo (846K): This model had No Audience Lock. It didn't look at our customer list; instead, it looked at every single person in Dortmund, Essen, and Dresden who was currently in a "High-Risk" segment.
The Difference: This methodology captured 750,000 extra people who never received a voucher. This "Open Market" approach makes it impossible to separate our campaign's impact from general city-wide trends.

2. The "Dormant Data Explosion" (The 724.8K Audience)
The Reality: In a massive market like Germany, there are hundreds of thousands of customers who haven't ordered in years.
The Error: V1 included all 724.8K Dormant/Churned users in its calculation. It "saw" the natural, organic return of a few thousand of these people and incorrectly attributed their orders to our voucher campaign.
The Result: This led to a "Hallucinated Uplift" of +8.9K orders in a segment where the Ground Truth proved only +3.8K were real.

3. The "Loyalist Noise" (The 122K vs. 45K Mismatch)
The Logic: V1 measured 122K Early/Engaged customers (instead of the 45K we actually targeted).
The Lesson: By including an extra 77,000 active loyalists, the model mistook their regular, everyday orders for "Incremental Growth." This is why V1 reported an impossible +30.7K incremental orders—it was simply counting people who were going to order anyway.

Note on Simulation Stability & Methodology Accuracy: 
While increasing the number of external model runs (e.g., 100x) and averaging the results is a common technique for reducing "random noise," it does not resolve the Systemic Bias identified in the City Look alike (Monte Carlo) methodology. The underlying Bayesian engine (Hamiltonian Monte Carlo) already executed thousands of internal iterations per run to find a statistically stable mean; therefore, the reported over-estimation (+35K IOV) is not a result of mathematical "jitter," but rather a fundamental failure in Audience Selection.
